The Problem

Health apps are full of numbers that don't mean anything without a reference point. You see 74 MS for HRV or 98% SpO₂ and have no idea if that's fine or something to worry about. The app we were building tracked 16 metrics — which made this problem worse, not better.

"I check my heart rate every morning and I still don't know if 72 is good for me or average or whatever."
— User interview, participant #4

Research & Competitive Analysis

Before designing, I analysed 15+ competitor apps across two categories: health monitoring apps (Whoop, Oura, Fitbit, Samsung Health) for feature benchmarking, and onboarding flows (Flo, Calm, Noom, Lifesum, Finch and 10+ others) for conversion patterns.

Finding 01 · Onboarding pattern

All top-converting apps (Flo, Noom, Calm) ask for the user's goal first — before asking for personal data. This creates a sense of personalisation and makes the subsequent paywall feel like a logical next step, not a gate. Apps that ask demographic data first felt transactional in testing.

Finding 02 · "We understand you" screen

After the user answers their goal, top apps show a confirmation screen: "We understand what you're aiming for — here's how we can help." This reduces anxiety before the paywall, increases empathy perception, and lifts conversion. Implemented in our onboarding as the "Creating Your Personal Heart Health Program" screen.

Finding 03 · Competitors gap — no camera measurement

None of the 15 analysed apps offered camera-based real-time pulse measurement without a wearable device. Whoop, Oura, and Fitbit all require hardware. This was identified as the key product differentiator — measure your heart health with just your iPhone, no device needed.

Finding 04 · Self-reflection questions (used selectively)

Only BodyWave and StressWatch used self-reflection questions in onboarding (e.g. "Do you want to know how your heart reacts to sleep?"). These increase emotional engagement but slow the flow. Research recommendation: use max 1–2 such questions, not a full quiz — implemented as the "Did you know" educational inserts between data collection steps.

Dark won +34% preference. Competitor benchmark (Whoop, Oura, Fitbit) all use dark. User testing showed light themes feel "clinical" for health data. Gradient background makes biometric data feel personal, not diagnostic.

07

Key Decisions

Four decisions that shaped the product. Not all of them were obvious at the time.

01
Dark theme — not an aesthetic choice
Competitors use dark UI because it signals premium. That's not why I chose it here. In testing, participants described light-themed health apps as "clinical" — and that word came up unprompted in 8 out of 12 sessions. The dark background with a warm gradient made the same data feel like it belonged to the user, not to a medical system. +34% preference over light, measured side-by-side.
"This feels like a premium health app, not like a hospital form. The dark background makes it feel personal." — Participant #7
Emotional tone · Research-driven
02
Normal ranges on every card — inline, not in a tooltip
The first version showed just the number. Round 1 testing: 7 out of 12 participants paused to Google what their reading meant. That's a UX failure, not a user knowledge gap. I added the normal range directly on the card — "Normal: 60–100" below the BPM value. Round 2: zero participants asked what their number meant. The fix was one line of text, not a new feature.
"Finally an app that tells me if my heart rate is actually normal. I don't have to Google it anymore." — Participant #3
Information design · Zero extra taps
03
Emoji mood tracker — the 1–10 scale had a 68% completion rate
The original spec had a numeric slider for mood. It tested fine in concept, but in practice people overthought it — "is today a 6 or a 7?" is a real cognitive load when you're doing a quick morning check-in. Five emoji don't require that decision. You glance, you tap. Completion rate went from 68% with the slider to 91% with emoji. I kept the same five emotional states, just removed the numbers.
"The emoji thing is so much easier — I just picked how I felt without thinking about it. I actually did it." — Participant #11
Micro-interaction · Completion rate
04
Camera measurement as a hard differentiator, not a nice-to-have
I went through 15 competitors during research. Every single one requires either a wearable or manual input. None of them measure pulse in real time through the camera. That gap wasn't a coincidence — it's technically harder to build. But from a product position it meant we could do something they literally can't. I pushed for it to become the primary measurement method, not a secondary option buried in settings.
Product strategy · Competitive gap
What didn't work in the first iteration

The first version of the Home screen had 5 visible metrics immediately. The thinking was: more data upfront = more value. In practice it read as overwhelming — participants slowed down at the entry point instead of getting past it. I cut the Home view to 2 primary metrics and moved the rest behind a tab. Engagement with the detail screens actually went up, not down, because users weren't already tired by the time they got there.

Key differentiator shipped

Camera-based real-time pulse measurement with no wearable required — the only feature none of the 15 analysed competitors offered. Users place their finger on the iPhone lens and the app reads BPM live. Identified as the primary product differentiator through competitive analysis gap mapping.

What I'd do differently

The calendar in History Tab was underused — users preferred the weekly strip on Home. I'd remove it and simplify. Also: research showed goal-first onboarding (Flo, Noom) converts better — I'd add "What's your main goal?" before the data collection questions in the onboarding flow.

09

Challenges

The hardest parts of this project weren't the UI — they were the decisions I had to make without enough data.

01
Health data has real thresholds — I had to get them right
Every "Normal: X–Y" range on the metric cards required actual medical sources, not just reasonable-sounding numbers. I spent more time verifying blood pressure thresholds, HRV ranges, and SpO₂ boundaries than I expected. Got sign-off from the client's medical advisor before anything shipped. It slowed the process down and was absolutely the right call.
Domain accuracy
02
The paywall before the product felt wrong — and I said so
12 out of 15 competitors put the paywall before users see the product. It's a proven conversion pattern. It also means users are being asked to pay for something they haven't experienced yet. I pushed for a "Creating Your Program" screen before the paywall — not to remove the paywall, but to give users a reason to subscribe before they hit it. Client approved it. We never got to A/B test it. That gap still bothers me.
Business vs user · Unresolved
03
16 metrics was the brief — progressive disclosure was my answer, not a simplification
I didn't get to cut the number of metrics. The brief was 16 minimum. So the question became: how do you organise 16 things so that the app doesn't feel like 16 things? Three layers: Home surfaces 2 metrics that matter daily, Wellness Tab holds the deeper data, History lives in its own tab for anyone who wants to track over time. Took 3 rounds of iteration before navigation became intuitive without guidance. The calendar component still didn't land well — users wanted a simple week strip, not a full calendar view.
Architecture · Progressive disclosure
One thing I kept thinking about

Health apps have an engagement problem that's different from most products. You're asking someone to open an app every day to look at data they don't fully understand, about a body they can't control. The design has to make that feel worthwhile, not anxious. Most of the decisions in this project were really about that — not about UI.
