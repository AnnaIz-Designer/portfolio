01 — Problem

The anxiety
of not knowing

People who track packages obsessively aren't anxious about their parcels being late. They're anxious about not knowing what "late" looks like — because no app tells them clearly enough to trust it.

"I checked the app six times yesterday. The status didn't change once. But I kept checking because I didn't feel like I could trust it."
— User interview, participant #6
What users experienced
  • ✕Status labels with zero context — "What does 'Clearance' mean?"
  • ✕No sense of progress — is 3 days normal or a delay?
  • ✕Different app per carrier — 4 apps for 4 packages
  • ✕Notification spam with no actionable information
  • ✕Nothing to do when something goes wrong
Research numbers
  • 4–6×Daily app checks — most with no status change between them
  • 3.2Average carrier apps installed per user with 2+ active deliveries
  • 54%Couldn't explain their current status in plain language — round 1 test
  • 8Competitor apps audited — none used colour + icon + progress simultaneously
02 — Design System

Five states.
Three channels.

Every status communicates through colour, icon, and progress bar position simultaneously. Any one alone is insufficient — colour-blind users miss colour-only cues, icons need context, progress bars without colour are abstract. All three together leave nothing to interpret.

📦
Packed
Progress 20%. Being prepared. System in motion, no action needed.
✈️
In transit
Progress 50%. En route. Plane icon signals movement. It's coming.
🚚
Pick up
Progress 75%. Action required. The only blue state — user must do something.
✅
Delivered
Progress 100%. Full bar. Green checkmark. Zero ambiguity.
⚠️
Exception
Red stops the eye. Progress bar replaced by explanation text.
Orange — system in motion
Packed + In transit. Something is happening but no action needed. Warm, active, not urgent.
Blue — action required
Pick up only. The only state where the user needs to do something. Distinct from orange by intent.
Red — stop and read
Exception only. Unlike orange which means "moving," red means "stopped and needs resolution."

04 — Decisions

What I chose
and why

01
Active / Archive split
Segmented control instead of filters
Early versions had a multi-filter system (by carrier, status, date). Testing showed users didn't want to filter — they wanted a clean split between "things I need to think about" and "things that are done." Two tabs. Active = needs attention. Archive = zero anxiety, nothing there requires action.
"I just want to know which ones I need to care about right now." — Participant #3
Information architecture
02
Progress bar as primary indicator
Visual position before text label
Most tracking apps show a text label and maybe a timeline below. I inverted the hierarchy: the progress bar is the first thing your eye hits. Colour tells you the state instantly, the label confirms it. Users scanned 4 cards in under 3 seconds in round 2 — without reading a single word to understand what was urgent.
Visual hierarchy · Pre-attentive processing
03
Exception breaks the template
Explanation text replaces the progress bar
When something goes wrong, showing a broken progress bar is useless. I replaced the progress component entirely with a text explanation: "Waiting for information from the courier." The exception state breaks from the card template deliberately. That break signals: this is different, pay attention.
"Finally — it tells me WHY it's stuck, not just that it is." — Participant #9
Error states · Clarity over consistency
04
Floating pill tab bar
3 actions, not 5
Standard iOS bottom nav bars have 4–5 tabs. This app has 3 functions: list, add parcel, settings. A floating pill with 3 buttons feels lighter and gives more visual space to the cards. The add button is orange (accent/primary) — the most important single action in the app, unmissable without being aggressive.
Navigation · iOS 26 patterns

Differentiator
Triple-channel status encoding (colour + icon + progress bar) — no competitor in the 8-app audit used all three simultaneously. Most use text labels with one colour. One used icons only with no colour differentiation. None had a progress bar that was also colour-coded.
What I'd do differently
The Exception state needs a CTA — "Contact carrier" or "Check courier website." I designed the explanation but left next steps implicit. 4 of 12 participants sat on the exception card without knowing what to do next. The design needs to close that loop.
06 — Challenges

What was
actually hard

The visual design was the easy part. The hard parts were structural — designing for things I couldn't control, and deciding how honest to be about what didn't work.

01
Statuses I couldn't control
Real carrier APIs return 50–200 different status codes. I designed for 5 clean states — but in production, those 5 would need to map intelligently to hundreds of raw inputs. The "Not found" state was added specifically because this gap exists: "We know you added this parcel, but we can't tell you anything yet." The most honest state in the app.
Technical reality
02
Blur + transparency failed on plain backgrounds
The card backdrop (rgba white 70% + blur) looks beautiful in the Figma mockup. On an older iPhone with a plain background, cards lost their visual separation. I added a subtle shadow and higher opacity fallback — but this is a real compromise. The aesthetic depends on the gradient background working as intended, which isn't always guaranteed.
Visual · Accessibility edge
03
Notes were added mid-project
The original brief didn't include notes. Halfway through, I added a note system. It required a separate interaction pattern that didn't fit the card-swipe model I'd built. I scoped notes as a secondary overlay rather than integrated — which works, but feels like an afterthought. It is. That's honest.
Scope creep
The takeaway

Tracking anxiety isn't about information density — it's about information trust. Users who checked 6 times a day weren't looking for more data. They were looking for a design that gave them permission to stop checking.
